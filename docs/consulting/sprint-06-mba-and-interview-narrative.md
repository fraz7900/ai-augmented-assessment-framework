# Sprint 6 — MBA and Consulting Interview Narrative

Continues the convention from Sprints 0-5: claims here are tied to what was actually built and found this sprint, listed in `docs/consulting/sprint-06-deliverables.md`'s Change Log.

## MBA Applications

**The project's own consulting-documentation discipline became a feature, not just a writing habit.** Every sprint since Sprint 0 has structured its deliverables doc as situation/complication/resolution — an analytical habit borrowed from case-team practice. Sprint 6 is the sprint where that same structure stopped being something applied *about* the product and became something the product *does*: `GET /assessments/{id}/dashboard` returns the identical structure this document is written in. Recognizing that a personal working discipline was itself a transferable design pattern, and building it into the system rather than leaving it as a documentation-only habit, is a specific, demonstrable instance of applying a consulting toolkit as engineering design, not just as prose style.

**A subtle statistical-honesty problem was caught before it shipped, not after.** The obvious way to summarize "how mature is this C2M2 assessment overall" is to average the ten domains' MIL scores into one number. That number would have looked clean and would have been wrong — MIL is an ordinal scale, and averaging ordinal categories produces a number with no real meaning, a mistake that shows up surprisingly often in dashboards built by teams that know their domain but haven't thought carefully about the measurement scale underneath it. Catching this before writing the summary function, rather than shipping the average and fixing it after a stakeholder misreads it, is the kind of quantitative judgment a case team is expected to bring into a client's KPI design work.

**Recognizing what the platform doesn't yet have a right to claim, and building the interface to say so instead of hiding it.** The resolution list needed some ordering principle; the honest one available (fewest missing practices) is not the same as the one a stakeholder might assume (business risk priority). Rather than presenting a bare ranked list and letting that assumption stand unchallenged, each item carries a one-sentence rationale stating the actual basis for its position. This is a small design choice with an outsized effect on whether the tool is trustworthy under scrutiny — the same instinct that makes a good case document state its assumptions rather than let a reader infer stronger ones than were actually tested.

## Consulting Interviews

**Consulting competency demonstrated:** designing a reporting layer the same way a case team designs a client-facing dashboard — decide what the headline number actually means before deciding how to compute it, rather than computing something convenient and writing a caption to explain it afterward. The MIL-averaging decision in ADR-0012 is exactly this: the measurement-scale question was resolved first, and the code follows from that answer, not the reverse.

**Business problem solved:** stakeholders across the project's own stakeholder map (CISO, compliance lead, internal audit) each need to answer "where are we short and what should we fix first" without manually cross-referencing two separate API responses (`/score` and `/evidence`) themselves. Sprint 6 collapses that into one call that already does the cross-referencing, grouped MECE by domain, with each gap's severity-relevant attribute (its MIL level) preserved and sorted rather than flattened into an undifferentiated list.

**Measurable value created:** a working, live-verified dashboard endpoint; a documented resolution to a genuine measurement-scale design question that a less careful build would have gotten wrong; 12 new tests, two of them full end-to-end proofs against real transcribed framework data rather than synthetic fixtures alone; and a data shape (`DashboardReport`) that directly de-risks Sprint 7's report-generation work by separating "what the report says" (already correct, tested, and shipped) from "what format it's rendered in" (the only remaining Sprint 7 problem).

**How this would be presented to a client:** as a working analytics layer delivered with an explicit statement of what it does and doesn't yet claim — real gap analysis grounded in real evidence, an honest count-based maturity summary instead of a misleading average, and effort-based (not fabricated risk-based) prioritization clearly labeled as such — the same way a case team would present an interim analysis with clearly marked assumptions rather than let a partial finding be read as more complete than it is.

## STAR story draft

**Situation:** The project needed its first true "report" surface — a dashboard translating dozens of practice-level evidence links into a leadership-ready gap analysis — and the obvious, fast way to summarize overall progress across a ten-domain framework was to average each domain's maturity score into one number.

**Task:** Design a dashboard that follows the project's established situation/complication/resolution consulting structure, presents an honest overall-progress figure, and prioritizes remaining gaps in a way a reviewer could actually act on — without either fabricating a business-impact score the platform has no data to support, or introducing a subtly wrong statistic (an average of an ordinal scale) that would look authoritative while being meaningless.

**Action:** Recognized before writing any summarization code that C2M2's MIL levels are ordinal, not continuous — a domain at MIL2 is not "twice as mature" as one at MIL1, so averaging them would misrepresent the underlying measurement. Designed two genuinely different summary paths dispatched on the framework's declared scoring model: a count-based headline ("N of M domains at MIL1+") for ordinal MIL frameworks, and a legitimate weighted-average fraction for NIST CSF 2.0's coverage model, where averaging a true fraction is mathematically sound. For the resolution list, chose an honest effort-based ranking (fewest missing practices first) over a fabricated impact score, with each item's rationale stating that basis explicitly rather than implying a stronger claim.

**Result:** A dashboard verified live against real data on both scoring models — a C2M2 demo that correctly prioritized a near-complete domain's last gap over a completely untouched, larger domain, and a NIST CSF 2.0 demo that confirmed the weighted-coverage path independently. 12 new tests, one ADR documenting both the measurement-scale reasoning and the effort-vs-impact framing decision, and a reporting layer that says exactly what it can support and nothing more.
